# Psychological Frameworks & 9 Triggers

**Complete guide to the 9 psychological triggers used throughout all copywriting agents**

---

## The 9 Psychological Triggers

These 9 triggers are applied throughout all copywriting agents. Understanding them deeply makes customization and advanced usage easier.

---

## 1. CURIOSITY

**Definition**: Creates an information gap the reader wants to fill

**How it works**: When you know part of a story but not all, your brain becomes motivated to find out the missing piece.

**Psychological basis**: The Loewenstein Curiosity Gap Theory. Humans are wired to close information gaps.

### Examples

**Weak**: "We have a course on copywriting"
**Strong**: "The $3k copywriter and the $30k copywriter write almost identical copy. So why does one get paid 10x more?"

**Why stronger**: Creates a gap ("I don't know the answer") that motivates reading.

### In Copywriting Agents

**copy-headlines** uses curiosity heavily
**copy-video** opens with curiosity hook (first 3 seconds)
**copy-social** applies curiosity to platform-native content

### Application Guide

```
Formula: [Statement of fact] + [Unexpected consequence] = Curiosity

Examples:
- "Everyone has a project management tool. So why is your team still drowning?"
- "99% of copywriters charge the same. The 1% earning 10x more don't."
- "You're already spending on tools. The question is: which tools actually save time?"
```

### Quality Checklist for Curiosity

- [ ] Information gap is clear (reader knows what they don't know)
- [ ] Gap is related to reader's situation
- [ ] Answer is worth finding out (not trivial)
- [ ] Natural desire to keep reading/watching

## 2. SPECIFICITY

**Definition**: Concrete details, numbers, and examples make claims credible

**How it works**: Vague claims feel like lies. Specific numbers feel true because they're hard to make up.

**Psychological basis**: Cognitive fluency and specificity effect. Our brains process specific claims as more trustworthy.

### Examples

**Weak**: "Improve your copywriting skills significantly"
**Strong**: "Move from $3k projects to $10k projects in 6 months"

**Why stronger**: The number $3k→$10k is specific and credible.

### In Copywriting Agents

**All agents** apply specificity
**copy-review** scores specificity as one of 7 points
**copy-analysis** teaches you to recognize specificity in great copy

### Application Guide

```
Specificity happens at multiple levels:

1. Numbers:
   ❌ "Save time" → ✅ "Save 10 hours per week"
   ❌ "Increase revenue" → ✅ "Increase revenue 23%"

2. Details:
   ❌ "Better project management" → ✅ "Eliminate daily standups for async teams"
   ❌ "Easier collaboration" → ✅ "One-click feedback loops instead of email chains"

3. Examples:
   ❌ "Works for companies" → ✅ "Works for 10-50 person engineering teams"
   ❌ "Trusted by leaders" → ✅ "Used by founders at Series B companies"

4. Timeframes:
   ❌ "Deliver results" → ✅ "Deliver results in 48 hours"
```

### Quality Checklist for Specificity

- [ ] Numbers used (not just percentages, but actual figures)
- [ ] Concrete examples provided (not abstract concepts)
- [ ] Details descriptive (not vague adjectives)
- [ ] Claims easy to believe (specific but not outlandish)

---

## 3. IDENTITY

**Definition**: Frames the reader's self-perception in a new way

**How it works**: People make decisions based on their identity. "I'm someone who..." shapes choices.

**Psychological basis**: Self-identity theory. People act consistently with their self-image.

### Examples

**Weak**: "You should learn positioning"
**Strong**: "You're not a cheap copywriter. You're an underpriced strategist."

**Why stronger**: Reframes identity. Now they see themselves differently (strategist not cheap).

### In Copywriting Agents

**copy-social** uses identity heavily
**copy-persona** helps identify key identity frames for audience
**copy-headlines** applies identity positioning

### Application Guide

```
Identity frame shifts:

From → To
- Struggling copywriter → Underpriced strategist
- Freelancer → Specialist consultant
- Solopreneur → Founder
- Student → Future expert
- Busy professional → Strategic leader
- Project manager → Team leader
- Manager → Executive
- Programmer → Problem solver
- Marketer → Growth operator

Formula: "You're not [old identity]. You're [new identity]."
```

### Quality Checklist for Identity

- [ ] New identity is aspirational (better than old one)
- [ ] New identity is accurate (not ridiculous)
- [ ] Shift changes how decision will be made
- [ ] Reader would want this new identity

---

## 4. CONTRAST

**Definition**: Showing two opposite things highlights differences

**How it works**: Contrast makes differences obvious and memorable. "This vs That" is persuasive.

**Psychological basis**: Figure-ground perception. We see what stands out against background.

### Examples

**Weak**: "Our tool is easy to set up"
**Strong**: "Other tools need 3 weeks to set up. Ours takes 3 minutes."

**Why stronger**: The contrast (3 weeks vs 3 minutes) is memorable and powerful.

### In Copywriting Agents

**copy-social** heavily uses Chief Neefe's contrast framework
**copy-headlines** uses contrast as primary trigger
**copy-video** uses visual and verbal contrast in hooks

### Application Guide

```
Contrast patterns:

Old Way vs New Way:
"Instead of scheduling meetings, you send one message."
"Instead of email chains, you get one-click feedback."

Price Contrast:
"You're paying $500/month for something that could cost $50/month."
"Everyone charges the same. The premium players charge different."

Speed Contrast:
"Used to take weeks. Now takes minutes."
"One action vs ten steps."

Outcome Contrast:
"Busier or more focused?"
"Drowning in tools or streamlined?"
```

### Quality Checklist for Contrast

- [ ] Both sides of contrast clearly stated
- [ ] Contrast is relevant to decision
- [ ] Difference is significant (not trivial)
- [ ] Preferred side is obvious from context

---

## 5. RELEVANCE

**Definition**: Speaking directly to the reader's specific situation

**How it works**: We pay attention to what's about us. Generic messages get ignored.

**Psychological basis**: Self-reference effect. We process information about ourselves preferentially.

### Examples

**Weak**: "This tool helps with project management"
**Strong**: "Engineering leads, you just lost 10 hours this week to meetings that could've been async. Here's how to get them back."

**Why stronger**: Specific to engineering leads and their exact pain.

### In Copywriting Agents

**All agents** must maintain relevance
**copy-persona** exists to ensure relevance
**copy-review** scores alignment (relevance metric)

### Application Guide

```
Relevance requires:

1. Specific audience named (not "people" or "businesses")
   ✅ "For 10-50 person engineering teams"
   ❌ "For companies"

2. Specific pain acknowledged (not generic problems)
   ✅ "You're drowning in async context"
   ❌ "You have collaboration challenges"

3. Specific consequence mentioned (not abstract benefits)
   ✅ "You'll have your evenings back"
   ❌ "Better work-life balance"

4. Specific situation addressed (not general)
   ✅ "When your team is distributed across 4 timezones"
   ❌ "For remote teams"
```

### Quality Checklist for Relevance

- [ ] Specific audience clearly identified
- [ ] Specific pain acknowledged (not generic)
- [ ] Consequence specific to their situation
- [ ] Could not apply to other audiences equally well

---

## 6. SCARCITY

**Definition**: Limited availability or uniqueness increases desire

**How it works**: Scarcity triggers FOMO (fear of missing out). Limited = valuable.

**Psychological basis**: Scarcity principle (Cialdini). We value rare things more.

### Examples

**Weak**: "Join our program"
**Strong**: "We take 20 students per cohort. Last cohort filled in 3 weeks. Next intake is Q1."

**Why stronger**: Creates scarcity (limited spots) and urgency (enrollment closing).

### In Copywriting Agents

**copy-headlines** applies scarcity strategically
**copy-social** uses scarcity in high-ticket positioning
**copy-analysis** helps identify scarcity in winning copy

### Application Guide

```
Scarcity types:

1. Limited availability:
   - "Only 20 spots per cohort"
   - "We work with 5 clients per quarter"
   - "Waitlist closed until Q1"

2. Deadline-based:
   - "Early bird pricing ends Friday"
   - "Next cohort starts in 14 days"
   - "This offer expires at midnight"

3. Exclusivity:
   - "Not for everyone (and that's intentional)"
   - "We turn down 90% of applicants"
   - "Only works for companies already doing X"

4. Rarity:
   - "Only one person has this combination of skills"
   - "This is the last time I'm offering this"
   - "This framework isn't taught anywhere else"
```

### Quality Checklist for Scarcity

- [ ] Scarcity is real (not made up)
- [ ] Scarcity is meaningful (not arbitrary)
- [ ] Scarcity creates genuine FOMO
- [ ] Scarcity doesn't feel like pressure tactic

---

## 7. AUTHORITY

**Definition**: Expert positioning and proof builds trust

**How it works**: We follow experts. Authority makes claims believable.

**Psychological basis**: Authority principle (Cialdini). We're wired to defer to experts.

### Examples

**Weak**: "I can help you improve your copywriting"
**Strong**: "I've written copy that generated $47M in revenue across 12 companies. Here's my portfolio."

**Why stronger**: Specific authority claim with concrete proof.

### In Copywriting Agents

**copy-research** validates authority of sources
**copy-social** positions authority in high-ticket copy
**copy-analysis** teaches how authority works in great copy

### Application Guide

```
Authority types:

1. Credentials:
   - "Certified by [recognized body]"
   - "PhD in [relevant field]"
   - "Published in [respected journal]"

2. Experience:
   - "15 years in industry"
   - "Worked with 500+ clients"
   - "Built company from $0 to $10M"

3. Proof:
   - "Clients see 40% revenue increase"
   - "Portfolio: [specific examples]"
   - "Case studies showing [results]"

4. Social proof:
   - "Trusted by [recognized companies]"
   - "1,000+ students completed program"
   - "Featured in [respected publications]"

5. Results:
   - "Generated $47M in client revenue"
   - "Won [industry award]"
   - "Published [book/course/framework]"
```

### Quality Checklist for Authority

- [ ] Authority is relevant to topic
- [ ] Authority is verifiable (not made up)
- [ ] Authority is specific (not vague)
- [ ] Authority actually addresses credibility gap

## 8. EMOTION

**Definition**: Tapping into feelings makes messages memorable and persuasive

**How it works**: We remember emotional content more. Emotion drives action.

**Psychological basis**: Affective heuristic. Feelings influence judgments more than logic.

### Examples

**Weak**: "You could improve your efficiency"
**Strong**: "Tired of explaining your value to clients who just want it cheaper? That's not a pricing problem. That's a positioning problem."

**Why stronger**: Taps into frustration and exhaustion (emotions).

### In Copywriting Agents

**copy-social** uses emotion throughout
**copy-video** opens with emotional hook
**copy-headlines** applies emotion strategically

### Application Guide

```
Emotional triggers:

Frustration:
- "Tired of [pain]?"
- "Frustrated by [situation]?"
- "Sick of [problem]?"

Aspiration:
- "Imagine having [desired state]"
- "Picture yourself [ideal outcome]"
- "What if you could [dream outcome]?"

Fear:
- "If you keep doing [current way], [bad outcome]"
- "The cost of inaction is..."
- "What happens if you don't [action]?"

Relief:
- "Finally, a [solution] for [problem]"
- "No more [pain]"
- "You don't have to [struggle] anymore"

Belonging:
- "Join the [community] of people who..."
- "Be part of the 1% who..."
- "Elite [group] understands..."
```

### Quality Checklist for Emotion

- [ ] Emotion is authentic (not manipulative)
- [ ] Emotion is relevant to decision
- [ ] Emotion doesn't override logic/reason
- [ ] Emotional appeal serves the main message

## 9. PATTERN INTERRUPT

**Definition**: Unexpected statement breaks habitual thinking

**How it works**: Our brain notices what's unexpected. Surprise makes us pay attention.

**Psychological basis**: Novelty detection. Brain prioritizes unexpected stimuli.

### Examples

**Weak**: "You should charge more for your services"
**Strong**: "You're not a cheap copywriter. You're an underpriced strategist."

**Why stronger**: Reframes situation unexpectedly (pattern interrupt).

### In Copywriting Agents

**copy-headlines** uses pattern interrupts heavily
**copy-social** applies pattern interrupts to stop scroll
**copy-video** opens with pattern interrupt (first 3 seconds)

### Application Guide

```
Pattern interrupt techniques:

Reversal:
- "Not [expected thing], [opposite thing]"
- "Instead of [assumption], [reality]"
- "You think [belief], but [truth]"

Contradiction:
- "[Statement 1]. [Contradictory statement 2]."
- "Everyone says [belief]. Here's why they're wrong."

Unexpected comparison:
- "Copywriting is like [surprising comparison]"
- "Your business is more like [unexpected analogy]"

Bold claim:
- "Most [group] are doing this wrong"
- "[Unexpected fact] will blow your mind"

Rule break:
- "Forget everything you learned about [topic]"
- "The rules don't apply anymore"

Specificity surprise:
- Claim so specific it seems made up (but isn't)
- Number so exact it demands attention
```

### Quality Checklist for Pattern Interrupt

- [ ] Interruption is unexpected (not predictable)
- [ ] Interruption is relevant (not random)
- [ ] Interruption serves a purpose (leads somewhere)
- [ ] Effect is attention-getting (not annoying)

## How Triggers Work Together

The most powerful copy uses **multiple triggers**:

### Example Headline (Using 4 Triggers)

**"The $3k copywriter and the $30k copywriter write the same stuff. So why does one get paid 10x more?"**

Breaking it down:

1. **Contrast** - $3k vs $30k (10x difference)
2. **Specificity** - Exact numbers, not "some" or "higher"
3. **Curiosity** - "Why?" gap that makes you want to read
4. **Relevance** - For copywriters stuck on pricing

All 4 triggers working together create powerful copy.

---

## Trigger Combinations

**High Power Combinations:**

1. **Curiosity + Specificity + Relevance**
   - "The 1% of copywriters earning 10x more use one strategy you haven't heard of"

2. **Contrast + Pattern Interrupt + Emotion**
   - "You think it's a pricing problem. It's actually a positioning problem. And that changes everything."

3. **Identity + Scarcity + Authority**
   - "Elite copywriters (the ones earning $50k/month) never compete on price. Here's why."

4. **Emotion + Relevance + Specificity**
   - "Tired of explaining your value to clients who want $500? The 47 who now charge $5k understood one thing..."

## Using This Framework

### For Customization:

When modifying agent prompts:
1. Verify triggers are accurately applied
2. Don't remove trigger applications (they're proven)
3. Add industry-specific examples that maintain triggers
4. Test custom versions against quality checklist

### For Learning:

When studying great copy:
1. Identify which triggers are at work
2. See how they combine
3. Notice which master copywriter's style
4. Learn why this combination works

### For Evaluation:

When copy-review scores output:
1. Scores on impact = how well triggers work
2. Scores on authenticity = triggers feel genuine
3. Scores on clarity = triggers understood clearly
4. Scores on alignment = triggers resonate with audience
